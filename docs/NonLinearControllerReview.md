# Comprehensive Review: Geometry Aware Robust Nonlinear Balance Controller

This document reviews the balance controller that currently lives in
[src/LegoBalance/NonLinearController.py](../src/LegoBalance/NonLinearController.py).
It is the second generation of the balancer for the LEGO SPIKE inverted
pendulum. The first generation was a model light composite variable tanh
law; that review has been fully replaced because the active controller is
now a geometry aware robust nonlinear controller that reasons in chassis
acceleration rather than in wheel velocity.

The review is intentionally long and keeps the derivations explicit so it
can serve as both an internal reference and the mathematical backbone of a
written report. Every symbol that appears in the equations is tied to a
specific config field, source file, or identified plant parameter so the
math and the code stay in one story.

---

## 1. The Physical System

### 1.1 Mechanical Description

The robot is a two wheel self balancing platform built on LEGO SPIKE Prime
hardware running Pybricks MicroPython. Physically, it is a roughly
rectangular body mounted on a single driven axle with two wheels. Yaw is
not actuated or stabilized; the controller only treats sagittal (fore aft)
balance, and the same scalar wheel velocity command is issued to the left
and right sides.

Although the robot is a box and not a point mass on a cart, its sagittal
balance dynamics are still governed by the same core unstable mechanism:
the center of mass lies above the axle, gravity creates a toppling torque,
and wheel motion creates the horizontal acceleration needed to catch the
falling body. What matters dynamically is only the location of the center
of mass above the axle, the pitch inertia of the body, and the horizontal
acceleration generated by wheel motion. The body shape matters through
the inertia and center of mass distance, not through the full 3D geometry,
so the reduced wheeled inverted pendulum model is still the right starting
point.

### 1.2 Canonical State Vector

The estimator (`src/LegoBalance/StateEstimator.py`) emits the canonical
balance state after applying sign correction, offset correction, and SI
unit conversion:

$$\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} \theta \\ \dot\theta \\ \phi \\ \dot\phi \end{bmatrix}$$

| Symbol | Meaning | Units |
|---|---|---|
| $\theta$ | Body tilt angle (0 = upright, + = forward lean) | rad |
| $\dot\theta$ | Body tilt rate | rad/s |
| $\phi$ | Mean wheel rotation angle (+ = rolled forward) | rad |
| $\dot\phi$ | Mean wheel rotation rate | rad/s |

Linear chassis position and velocity are derived on demand from the wheel
radius:

$$p = r\,\phi, \qquad \dot p = r\,\dot\phi$$

Throughout this document, `p` and `pDot` are views of the wheel state, not
independent state variables. The controller never integrates them.

### 1.3 Single Scalar Command

The control output is a symmetric wheel velocity command applied equally
to both wheels:

$$u_{\text{left}} = u_{\text{right}} = u$$

with positive `u` meaning "drive the chassis forward" after the
configured `motors.forwardSign` correction has been applied by the
hardware adapter. Left/right symmetry is enforced by construction in
`Compute`, not by a tuning choice.

### 1.4 Inherent Instability Timescale

The gravitational instability is set by

$$\alpha = \frac{mgl}{I + ml^2}$$

where `m = chassis.bodyMass`, `l = chassis.bodyHeightCoM`, and
`I = chassis.bodyInertia`. With the current
[configs/Default.yaml](../configs/Default.yaml) values
($m = 0.409$ kg, $l = 0.075$ m, $I = 0.000637$ kg m$^2$):

$$\alpha = \frac{0.409 \cdot 9.81 \cdot 0.075}{0.000637 + 0.409 \cdot 0.075^2}
        = \frac{0.3010}{0.002937} \approx 102.5 \;\text{rad/s}^2$$

$$T_\text{diverge} = 1/\sqrt\alpha \approx 0.099 \;\text{s}$$

That is the natural time for the body to double its tilt angle from rest
under gravity alone. With a 100 Hz control loop (10 ms period) the
controller only has about 10 samples before an uncorrected lean becomes
serious. The actuator timescale set by the identified lag `tau = 0.25 s`
is slower than this unstable body timescale, which is the structural
mismatch the new controller is designed around. Compare this to the
earlier build ($m = 0.276$, $l = 0.105$, $I = 0.00337$) where
$\alpha \approx 44.3$ rad/s$^2$ and $T_\text{diverge} \approx 0.15$ s:
the new chassis is shorter and heavier, and therefore even more unstable
per unit of control authority than the old one.

## 2. Why The Old Composite Variable Tanh Law Plateaued

The earlier controller implemented

$$s = k_\theta \,\text{db}(\theta - \theta_\text{target})
    + k_{\dot\theta} \,\text{db}(\dot\theta)
    - k_\phi \phi - k_{\dot\phi} \dot\phi$$

$$u = u_\max \cdot \tanh(s / s_\text{scale})$$

with a hard saturation backstop, an optional IIR on tilt rate, and a
small post hoc lag compensator on the wheel state part of the law.

That design was a smooth bounded sliding mode cousin and it did stabilize
the upright neighborhood. In bench tests it kept the robot vertical as
long as nothing external happened to it. The practical failure mode was
consistent and informative:

> The robot could stay upright under quiet conditions, but even a slight
> touch (and sometimes no touch at all, just a battery droop) was enough
> to make it lose balance and fall.

That symptom is not a tuning plateau. It is a structural plateau. The
controller relied on the following assumptions:

1. the control channel (wheel velocity) reacts to commands quickly
   compared to the fast unstable tilt dynamics, and
2. the state surface $s$ captures all directions of recovery at once, so
   inner loop balance and outer loop drift suppression can share one
   fast gain schedule.

Both assumptions are wrong for this hardware. The identified actuator
lag `tau ~ 0.20 to 0.25 s` is noticeably slower than the unstable body
timescale `T_diverge ~ 0.1 s`, so the motor never produces the wheel
velocity the fast law tells it to produce in time. And collapsing the
full state into a single flat surface prevented the slow drift
suppression loop from being tuned independently of the fast anti fall
loop, so strong wheel position terms destabilized the balance path while
weak wheel position terms let the robot walk away.

The fix is not larger gains. It is a structural change: reason in
chassis acceleration first, then map that acceleration through the
actuator model to a wheel velocity command, and separate the slow
recentering loop from the fast tilt loop so each one can be tuned
independently.

## 3. Reduced Sagittal Plane Plant Model

### 3.1 Nominal Reduced Tilt Dynamics

Let

- $m$ = `chassis.bodyMass`
- $l$ = `chassis.bodyHeightCoM` (vertical distance from axle to body CoM)
- $I$ = `chassis.bodyInertia` (pitch inertia about the body CoM)
- $r$ = `chassis.wheelRadius`
- $p = r\phi$ = chassis linear position
- $g = 9.81$ m/s$^2$

Treating the body as a rigid pendulum about the axle with parallel axis
term $ml^2$, the sagittal tilt equation becomes

$$(I + ml^2)\,\ddot\theta \;=\; mgl\sin\theta \;-\; ml\,\ddot p \;+\; d_\theta$$

where $d_\theta$ lumps every unmodeled effect: friction at the axle,
contact with the ground, wheel slip, encoder noise, and parameter
mismatch.

Defining the reduced pitch inertia about the axle and two normalized
parameters,

$$J = I + ml^2, \qquad \hat\alpha = \frac{mgl}{J}, \qquad \hat\beta = \frac{ml}{J}$$

the reduced dominant tilt dynamics become

$$\ddot\theta \;=\; \hat\alpha \sin\theta \;-\; \hat\beta\,a \;+\; \Delta_\theta \tag{3.1}$$

with $a = \ddot p$ the chassis linear acceleration and $\Delta_\theta$ the
normalized uncertainty term. Equation (3.1) is the single most important
equation in this document: it is the plant the new controller actually
reasons against.

### 3.2 Numerical Values From The Active Config

Using the current Default.yaml values:

| Parameter | Value | Units |
|---|---|---|
| $m$ | 0.409 | kg |
| $l$ | 0.075 | m |
| $I$ (CoM) | 0.000637 | kg m$^2$ |
| $r$ | 0.0285 | m |
| $J = I + ml^2$ | 0.002937 | kg m$^2$ |
| $\hat\alpha = mgl/J$ | 102.5 | rad/s$^2$ |
| $\hat\beta = ml/J$  | 10.45 | 1/m |

The `betaHat` value tells you how much chassis linear acceleration you
need to produce a given restoring effect on tilt. A positive `aDes` of
$1$ m/s$^2$ produces approximately $10.4$ rad/s$^2$ of restoring tilt
acceleration, which at small angles is enough to quickly win against
$\hat\alpha \sin\theta$ for tilt errors out to about 80 mrad (4.5 deg)
with only 1 m/s$^2$ of commanded acceleration.

### 3.3 Handling Inertia Uncertainty

The pitch inertia is the weakest parameter in the reduced model. It
depends on LEGO element layout, cabling, battery seating, and mass
distribution details that are not realistically known to better than
$\pm 30\%$ even after careful measurement. The controller treats this
honestly: it uses the configured nominal $I$ to build $\hat\alpha$ and
$\hat\beta$ for the nominal law, and then lets the smooth robust
correction (Section 6) absorb the mismatch.

If `chassis.bodyInertia` is zero or missing, the controller falls back
to the rectangular body approximation

$$\hat I = \frac{m}{12}\left(L^2 + H^2\right)$$

where $L = $ `chassis.bodyLength` and $H = $ `chassis.bodyHeight`. This
fallback is explicitly marked as an estimate both in the config comments
and in the source code. If neither the pitch inertia nor both box
dimensions are provided, `NonLinearController.__init__` raises a
`ValueError` rather than silently inventing a constant. This
fail loud behavior is the correct tradeoff when a wrong value can
destabilize the closed loop.

## 4. Actuator Model And Why It Is Central

### 4.1 The Plant The Controller Actually Sees

Pybricks does not expose a torque interface. `Motor.run(...)` accepts a
wheel angular velocity command and runs its own internal velocity servo
on top of the raw voltage output. From the balance controller's
viewpoint, the control input is therefore wheel angular velocity, not
torque.

The Port F velocity sweep documented in
[MotorActuatorLagIdentification.md](MotorActuatorLagIdentification.md)
and recorded by `hub/HubSingleMotorStepResponseF.py` fits a first order
response in wheel angular velocity:

$$\frac{d\dot\phi_{\text{actual}}}{dt} \;=\; \frac{u - \dot\phi}{\tau} \tag{4.1}$$

The identified nominal time constant is $\tau \approx 0.20$ to $0.25$ s.
The current config uses `actuatorTau = 0.25`. This value is an
engineering estimate, not a precision identification: the step response
was read from a plot rather than fit to raw telemetry, and the effective
$\tau$ very likely depends on operating point, battery droop, and
loading. The point of the robust correction in the inner loop is to
tolerate this kind of uncertainty in $\tau$ without retuning.

### 4.2 From Wheel Acceleration To Chassis Acceleration

Substituting $p = r\phi$ into (4.1) and differentiating gives

$$\ddot p \;=\; r\,\ddot\phi \;\approx\; \frac{r}{\tau}\,\bigl(u - \dot\phi\bigr) \tag{4.2}$$

and therefore

$$a \;=\; \ddot p \;\approx\; \frac{r}{\tau}(u - \dot\phi) \tag{4.3}$$

Equation (4.3) says: with the actuator model, the instantaneous chassis
linear acceleration is proportional to the difference between the
commanded and measured wheel angular velocity, scaled by the wheel
radius over the lag time constant.

### 4.3 Inversion: Desired Acceleration To Wheel Velocity Command

Rearranging (4.3) gives the inversion formula:

$$\boxed{\;u \;=\; \dot\phi \;+\; \frac{\tau}{r}\,a_{\text{des}}\;} \tag{4.4}$$

This is the bridge between the physics based inner loop design and the
wheel velocity command the Pybricks motor API actually accepts. Without
it, the controller would command a wheel velocity whose steady state the
actuator never actually produces, and the effective chassis acceleration
would always be some small fraction of the commanded one.

The implementation uses this mapping directly in
`NonLinearController._MapAccelerationToVelocityCommand`, and the current
velocity $\dot\phi$ it substitutes is the measured wheel rate straight
from the state vector. If `actuatorTau <= 0` the mapping is undefined
and the method falls back to returning the current wheel speed so the
command is at least well defined. Configurations that want the new law
to work at all must therefore provide a strictly positive
`actuatorTau`.

## 5. Controller Architecture Overview

The new controller has three layers, each with its own timescale and
responsibility:

```
                   +----------------------------------------------+
                   |                Outer loop (slow)              |
phi, phiDot  --->  |   thetaRef = targetTilt - sat(kP*p + kD*pDot) |
                   +----------------------------------------------+
                               |     |       thetaRef, thetaRefDot
                               v     v
                   +----------------------------------------------+
                   |                Inner loop (fast)              |
theta, thetaDot -> |   Lyapunov style aNom(e, eDot)                |
                   |      + smooth robust correction aRob(s)       |
                   |      = aDes                                   |
                   +----------------------------------------------+
                                     |     aDes (m/s^2)
                                     v
                   +----------------------------------------------+
                   |          Actuator lag inversion               |
phiDot       --->  |   u = phiDot + (tau/r) * aDes                 |
                   +----------------------------------------------+
                                     |     u (rad/s)
                                     v
                   +----------------------------------------------+
                   |          Hard symmetric saturation            |
                   |   uBound = sat(u, maxWheelRate)               |
                   +----------------------------------------------+
                                     |
                                     v
                              ControlOutput(uBound, uBound, Velocity, t)
```

The separation matters. The old controller tried to do all three things
at once inside the same scalar surface. The new controller keeps them
conceptually and dynamically separate: the outer loop is slow and never
dominates the fast anti fall logic, the inner loop reasons about chassis
acceleration with a principled Lyapunov style law, and the actuator
inversion is the only place where the command interface enters.

## 6. Inner Loop: Lyapunov Style Nominal Law

### 6.1 Error Coordinates

The inner loop regulates the body tilt relative to the slow reference
produced by the outer loop:

$$e \;=\; \theta - \theta_\text{ref}, \qquad
\dot e \;=\; \dot\theta_\text{used} - \dot\theta_\text{ref} \tag{6.1}$$

where $\dot\theta_\text{used}$ is either the raw tilt rate from the
estimator, or a lightly IIR filtered version when
`thetaDotFilterAlpha > 0`. In the current config this filter is active
at $\alpha_f = 0.7$, which gives roughly a 7 Hz first order cutoff at
the 100 Hz loop rate and is used to soften noise from the IMU rate
channel rather than to add damping.

### 6.2 Candidate Energy Function

Choose

$$V(e, \dot e) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\dot e^2 \;+\; \lambda\bigl(1 - \cos e\bigr), \qquad \lambda > 0 \tag{6.2}$$

This is an energy like function built from the pendulum potential
structure. For small angles $(1 - \cos e) \approx e^2/2$, so near
upright

$$V(e, \dot e) \;\approx\; \tfrac{1}{2}\dot e^2 + \tfrac{\lambda}{2} e^2$$

which is the usual quadratic Lyapunov candidate from linear systems.
The difference at larger tilt is that the potential term saturates at
$2\lambda$ rather than growing without bound, which is physically more
consistent with a real pendulum.

### 6.3 Lyapunov Derivative

Taking the time derivative of (6.2) and ignoring slow reference
acceleration effects,

$$\dot V \;=\; \dot e\,\ddot e \;+\; \lambda \sin e \cdot \dot e \;=\; \dot e\bigl(\ddot e + \lambda \sin e\bigr) \tag{6.3}$$

From the plant equation (3.1) with $e \approx \theta$ (valid when the
reference and its derivative are small or slow), the tilt error
acceleration satisfies

$$\ddot e \;\approx\; \hat\alpha \sin e - \hat\beta\,a + \Delta_\theta \tag{6.4}$$

### 6.4 Nominal Desired Acceleration

Picking the nominal desired chassis acceleration to make $\dot V$
negative semidefinite in the nominal case:

$$a_\text{nom} \;=\; \frac{(\hat\alpha + \lambda)\sin e \;+\; k_D\,\dot e}{\hat\beta} \tag{6.5}$$

Substituting (6.5) into (6.4) and then (6.4) into (6.3) gives, in the
ideal case and with the nominal parameters exactly right and the
uncertainty $\Delta_\theta$ vanishing,

$$\dot V \;=\; -k_D\,\dot e^2 \;\le\; 0$$

which is the desired dissipative behavior. The closed loop error
dynamics in the small angle limit become

$$\ddot e + k_D\,\dot e + \lambda\,e \;\approx\; 0 \tag{6.6}$$

This is a standard second order linear system. The tuning is therefore
natural in classical terms:

$$\lambda = \omega_n^2, \qquad k_D = 2\zeta\omega_n \tag{6.7}$$

with $\omega_n$ = `innerNaturalFrequency` and $\zeta$ =
`innerDampingRatio`. The current config uses
$\omega_n = 5$ rad/s and $\zeta = 1$, which gives a critically damped
inner loop with a time constant of roughly $1/\omega_n = 0.2$ s. That
is slower than the natural unstable timescale ($\sim 0.1$ s), which is
a deliberate choice: the outer fast dynamics come from the robust
correction, not from the nominal law.

### 6.5 Why This Is Better Than Plain Feedback Linearization

A pure feedback linearization approach would pick

$$a_\text{FL} \;=\; \frac{1}{\hat\beta}\bigl(\hat\alpha \sin e + v\bigr)$$

and then stabilize the resulting linear chain with a linear feedback
$v$. That is mathematically elegant and it would cancel the gravity
term exactly if $\hat\alpha$ and $\hat\beta$ were correct. But on LEGO
hardware neither is correct to better than about 30%, the actuator is
not a torque source, and the wheel ground contact is uncertain. A pure
inversion therefore cancels a value that is not actually the true
gravity term, and the residual error propagates into a persistent
disturbance on the linear chain.

The nominal Lyapunov law avoids this failure mode by using
$(\hat\alpha + \lambda)\sin e$ rather than $\hat\alpha\sin e$ alone.
The extra $\lambda\sin e$ term is a stabilizing virtual gravity, which
means small errors in $\hat\alpha$ do not destroy the nominal descent
property as long as $\lambda$ dominates the parameter uncertainty. The
actual robustness against uncertainty, however, is supplied by the
separate smooth robust correction below, not by the nominal law.

## 7. Smooth Robust Correction

### 7.1 Sliding Surface Variable

Define a smooth sliding surface like variable that mixes the tilt error
rate and a nonlinear contribution from the tilt error itself:

$$s_\sigma \;=\; \dot e \;+\; c_s \sin e \tag{7.1}$$

with $c_s$ = `surfaceGain`. The $\sin e$ term makes the surface react
to tilt errors even when the error rate briefly vanishes (for example
at the top of a corrective swing), which would otherwise stall the
robust term exactly when it is most needed.

### 7.2 Smooth Sliding Mode Correction

A classical sliding mode law would add

$$a_\text{rob}^\text{SM} \;=\; k_r \cdot \text{sign}(s_\sigma) / \hat\beta$$

which provides maximum corrective acceleration on either side of the
surface and guarantees finite time convergence under perfect actuation.
In practice the `sign` function is discontinuous, and feeding a
discontinuous input through the first order actuator lag produces
chatter, noise injection, and thermal stress on the motor. Worse, the
lag means `sign(s)` is already too late: by the time the sign flips,
the actual wheel velocity is still catching up to the previous command.

The new controller replaces `sign` with a smooth `tanh` through a
boundary layer:

$$a_\text{rob} \;=\; \frac{k_r}{\hat\beta}\,\tanh\!\left(\frac{s_\sigma}{\varepsilon}\right) \tag{7.2}$$

with $k_r$ = `robustGain` and $\varepsilon$ =
`boundaryLayerWidth`. Inside the boundary layer ($|s_\sigma| \ll
\varepsilon$) the law behaves like a high gain linear damping term in
the composite variable; outside it saturates at $\pm k_r / \hat\beta$.

### 7.3 Total Desired Chassis Acceleration

$$\boxed{\;a_\text{des} \;=\; a_\text{nom} \;+\; a_\text{rob}\;} \tag{7.3}$$

Two implementation invariants are enforced in the code:

- the robust term is always smooth: it uses `tanh`, never `sign`,
- `boundaryLayerWidth` is strictly positive (validated in
  `__init__`).

Together these guarantee the command is continuous even under pure
sliding behavior, which matches what the lagged actuator can actually
track.

### 7.4 Relation To Classical Nonlinear Control Families

| Approach | Key idea | Relation to this controller |
|---|---|---|
| **Feedback linearization** | Cancel $\hat\alpha\sin\theta$ exactly | Deliberately avoided: parameters are too uncertain for clean cancellation |
| **Sliding mode control (discontinuous)** | Discontinuous law on $s = 0$ surface | Sliding surface shape is reused, but the discontinuous law is unsafe with the lagged actuator, so `tanh` replaces `sign` |
| **Boundary layer sliding mode** | `sat(s/eps)` instead of `sign(s)` | Equivalent in spirit; `tanh` is the infinitely differentiable version and is chosen here for smoothness |
| **Control Lyapunov Function (CLF)** | Design $u$ so that $\dot V < 0$ along trajectories | The nominal law is derived in exactly this style; the robust term is then added on top rather than from inside the CLF proof |
| **Linear state feedback (LQR, pole placement)** | $u = -K\mathbf{x}$ from an LTI model | Recovered near upright when $e$ and $\dot e$ are small and the robust term is inside its boundary layer; the new law reduces to an LTI feedback in that regime |

The final controller is therefore best described as **a CLF style
nominal law with a smooth sliding mode robust correction, mapped
through an identified first order actuator model**. No single classical
family by itself produces the full design.

## 8. Outer Loop: Slow Position Recentering

### 8.1 Motivation

The inner loop is enough to keep the robot upright, but it has no
preferred chassis position. Without an outer loop, small residual bias
in `targetTilt`, small motor asymmetry, or battery droop causes the
robot to slowly walk away, even while balanced. That drift is
unacceptable on a bench.

The outer loop adds a slow position recentering mechanism by biasing
the tilt reference itself. Physically, if the robot has drifted
forward, the outer loop asks the inner loop to briefly lean the body
backward, which makes the body fall backward slightly, which in turn
makes the inner loop pedal the wheels backward to catch the fall. Net
motion: the robot returns toward the origin.

### 8.2 Reference Offset

Linear chassis position and velocity are derived from the wheel state:

$$p = r\phi, \qquad \dot p = r\dot\phi$$

Raw offset:

$$\theta_{\text{ref,offset,raw}} \;=\; -\bigl(k^\text{out}_p \, p \;+\; k^\text{out}_d \, \dot p\bigr) \tag{8.1}$$

with $k^\text{out}_p$ = `outerPositionGain` (rad/m) and
$k^\text{out}_d$ = `outerVelocityGain` (rad/(m/s)). The sign is such
that positive drift forward asks for negative tilt reference (body
leans backward), which drives the wheels backward through the inner
loop.

Saturated offset:

$$\theta_\text{ref,offset} \;=\; \text{sat}\bigl(\theta_\text{ref,offset,raw},\; \theta_\text{ref,max}\bigr) \tag{8.2}$$

with $\theta_\text{ref,max}$ = `maxReferenceTiltOffset`. The saturation
is important: without it a large $p$ could ask for an enormous lean
that the inner loop could never track, and the resulting mismatch would
destabilize the system.

Full reference tilt:

$$\theta_\text{ref} \;=\; \theta_0 \;+\; \theta_\text{ref,offset} \tag{8.3}$$

where $\theta_0$ = `targetTilt` is the existing configured static bias
used to absorb assembly asymmetry and any residual IMU zero mismatch.
In the current config `targetTilt = 0.0`, which means the outer loop
recentering term is the only authority over the steady lean. This
simplifies the interpretation: if the robot walks, raise
`outerPositionGain`; do not chase `targetTilt`.

### 8.3 Reference Derivative Without An Algebraic Loop

The inner loop needs $\dot\theta_\text{ref}$ so it can form
$\dot e = \dot\theta_\text{used} - \dot\theta_\text{ref}$. Formally,
differentiating (8.3) with respect to time and using
$\dot p = r\dot\phi$, $\ddot p = a$,

$$\dot\theta_\text{ref} \;=\; -k^\text{out}_p \,\dot p \;-\; k^\text{out}_d \, a \tag{8.4}$$

which looks circular because $a = a_\text{des}$ is exactly what the
inner loop is trying to compute. Closing that algebraic loop would
introduce a direct feedthrough from the new command to the new
reference derivative and could amplify noise.

The implementation breaks the loop by estimating the *previous*
acceleration using the actuator model and the previous bounded command:

$$a_\text{est,prev} \;=\; \frac{r}{\tau}\bigl(u_\text{prev} - \dot\phi\bigr) \tag{8.5}$$

$$\dot\theta_\text{ref} \;=\; -k^\text{out}_p \,\dot p \;-\; k^\text{out}_d \, a_\text{est,prev} \tag{8.6}$$

If `tau <= 0` the mapping is degenerate; the code falls back to
$a_\text{est,prev} = 0$ and drops the $k^\text{out}_d \, a$ term. That
fallback is an accepted minor loss of feed forward that only matters
when someone forgets to set the actuator time constant.

Note that (8.6) is the derivative of the *unclamped* reference. When
the offset is actively saturated (that is, when
$|\theta_\text{ref,offset,raw}| > \theta_\text{ref,max}$), the code is
intentionally a little looser on the feed forward: the saturation freeze
is not differentiated. That asymmetry is acceptable because the outer
loop is slow by design and the inner loop absorbs the small mismatch.

## 9. Full Control Step

The complete `Compute(state)` pipeline on each iteration is:

```
1.  if not state.valid: return stop
    if any component of state is NaN: return stop
2.  thetaDotUsed = filter_or_passthrough(thetaDot, filterAlpha)
3.  p       = r * phi
    pDot    = r * phiDot
4.  aEstPrev = (r/tau) * (uPrev - phiDot)   if tau > 0 else 0
5.  offsetRaw    = -(kOuterP * p + kOuterD * pDot)
    offset       = sat(offsetRaw, maxReferenceTiltOffset)
    thetaRef     = targetTilt + offset
    thetaRefDot  = -(kOuterP * pDot + kOuterD * aEstPrev)
6.  e     = theta - thetaRef
    eDot  = thetaDotUsed - thetaRefDot
7.  J        = I + m * l^2
    alphaHat = m * g * l / J
    betaHat  = m * l / J
8.  aNom = ((alphaHat + lambda) * sin(e) + kDamping * eDot) / betaHat
9.  s        = eDot + cSurface * sin(e)
    aRobust  = (kRobust / betaHat) * tanh(s / epsilonBoundary)
    aDes     = aNom + aRobust
10. uRaw    = phiDot + (tau / r) * aDes
    uBound  = sat(uRaw, maxWheelRate)
11. store uBound as uPrev for the next call
12. return ControlOutput(uBound, uBound, Velocity, timestamp)
```

Steps 7a (the geometry constants) are actually computed once in
`__init__` rather than every step; they are listed here to make the
physical story explicit. Every other step runs per control iteration.

| (8.1)-(8.3) $\theta_\text{ref}$ | `_ComputeReferenceTilt` | `outerPositionGain`, `outerVelocityGain`, `maxReferenceTiltOffset`, `targetTilt` |
| (8.6) $\dot\theta_\text{ref}$   | `_ComputeReferenceTiltDerivative` | same + `aEstPrev` |
| hard saturation            | `_ClampCommand` via `SaturateSymmetric` | `maxWheelRate` |
| state persistence          | `Compute`, `Reset` | `_previousCommand`, `_thetaDotFiltered` |

The controller keeps exactly two persistent internal states across
calls: the IIR filter memory $\dot\theta_\text{filtered}$ and the
previous bounded command $u_\text{prev}$. `Reset()` clears both to
zero so the first call after a reset behaves exactly like the first
call after construction.

## 11. Stability Analysis

### 11.1 Linearized Small Signal Behavior Near Upright

Near upright and near the outer loop reference, write $e = \theta -
\theta_\text{ref} \approx \theta$ and $\dot e \approx \dot\theta$,
assume the robust correction is inside its boundary layer so that
$\tanh(s_\sigma/\varepsilon) \approx s_\sigma/\varepsilon$, and
linearize:

$$a_\text{nom} \;\approx\; \frac{(\hat\alpha + \lambda)e + k_D \dot e}{\hat\beta}$$

$$a_\text{rob} \;\approx\; \frac{k_r}{\hat\beta\,\varepsilon}\bigl(\dot e + c_s e\bigr)$$

Total:

$$a_\text{des} \;\approx\;
\underbrace{\frac{(\hat\alpha + \lambda)}{\hat\beta}}_{K_e^{(n)}}\,e
\;+\; \underbrace{\frac{k_D}{\hat\beta}}_{K_{\dot e}^{(n)}}\,\dot e
\;+\; \underbrace{\frac{k_r c_s}{\hat\beta\,\varepsilon}}_{K_e^{(r)}}\,e
\;+\; \underbrace{\frac{k_r}{\hat\beta\,\varepsilon}}_{K_{\dot e}^{(r)}}\,\dot e$$

Combining the nominal and robust linearizations, the total effective
linear error feedback gains near upright are

$$K_e \;=\; \frac{\hat\alpha + \lambda + k_r c_s / \varepsilon}{\hat\beta},
\qquad K_{\dot e} \;=\; \frac{k_D + k_r/\varepsilon}{\hat\beta}$$

Substituting into the closed loop tilt dynamics (3.1) with
$a = \hat\beta \cdot a_\text{des}/\hat\beta = \hat\beta K_e e + \hat\beta K_{\dot e} \dot e$
and the plant coupling $-\hat\beta a$:

$$\ddot e \;\approx\; \hat\alpha e - \hat\beta (K_e e + K_{\dot e} \dot e)$$

$$\ddot e \;+\; \hat\beta K_{\dot e}\,\dot e \;+\; (\hat\beta K_e - \hat\alpha)\,e \;\approx\; 0$$

Substituting the gains:

$$\ddot e \;+\; (k_D + k_r/\varepsilon)\,\dot e \;+\; (\lambda + k_r c_s/\varepsilon)\,e \;\approx\; 0 \tag{11.1}$$

Two observations fall out of (11.1):

- the nominal $\hat\alpha$ from the plant cancels against the
  $\hat\alpha + \lambda$ term in the control law, leaving only
  $\lambda$ (plus the robust contribution). This is the structural
  reason the inner loop does not need exact knowledge of $\hat\alpha$:
  it appears additively in both the plant and the law, and the nominal
  design cancels it.
- both the stiffness and the damping of the linearized closed loop are
  boosted by the robust term through its linearization inside the
  boundary layer. This is why raising `robustGain` feels like adding
  both stiffness and damping at the same time near upright.

### 11.2 Routh-Hurwitz Sanity Check

For the second order system (11.1) to be stable near upright, both
coefficients must be positive:

$$k_D + k_r/\varepsilon \;>\; 0, \qquad \lambda + k_r c_s/\varepsilon \;>\; 0$$

With the current config ($k_D = 2\zeta\omega_n = 10$, $\lambda = \omega_n^2 = 25$, $k_r = 2$, $\varepsilon = 0.8$, $c_s = 3$),
both coefficients are strictly positive by construction. There is no
conservative case where either can become negative while the robust
correction is enabled, so the nominal small signal loop is
unconditionally stable in this regime.

### 11.3 Global Stability, And What Is Not Proven

Equation (11.1) is a local analysis. It does not prove global stability
and it does not prove convergence outside a neighborhood of upright. A
full proof would require:

1. extending the Lyapunov analysis from Section 6 to include the
   nonlinear plant (3.1) rather than the linearization,
2. bounding the unmodeled uncertainty $\Delta_\theta$ and showing the
   robust term dominates it,
3. including the actuator dynamics in the proof rather than treating
   the inversion as perfect, and
4. accounting for the outer loop feedback through $\dot\theta_\text{ref}$.

None of that is done formally. What the controller does have is:

- a principled nominal Lyapunov descent proof (Section 6.3),
- a smooth robust backup that tolerates bounded uncertainty in the
  boundary layer sense (Section 7),
- an explicit actuator inversion that respects the first order lag
  (Section 4.3),
- a slow outer loop that does not violate the timescale separation
  assumption (Section 8),
- linearized stability near the operating point (Section 11.2).

This is the appropriate level of rigor for an educational LEGO
platform: strong structure, honest nominal math, and conservative
robust margins. The controller is not claimed to be globally provably
stable, and should not be described as such in a report.

### 11.4 Region Of Attraction Estimate

As a practical upper bound on the region of attraction, the largest
tilt error the controller can recover from is limited by the maximum
chassis acceleration the actuator can deliver, which is approximately

$$|a_\text{max}| \;\approx\; \frac{r}{\tau}\,u_\text{max}
\;=\; \frac{0.0285}{0.25}\cdot 17.44
\;\approx\; 1.99 \;\text{m/s}^2$$

This upper bound on the effective chassis acceleration is independent
of the control law and cannot be exceeded no matter how aggressively
the inner loop asks for corrective acceleration. Equating
$\hat\beta\,|a_\text{max}|$ to $\hat\alpha\,|e|$ gives the largest
static tilt error the actuator can possibly overcome:

$$|e|_\text{max,static} \;\approx\; \frac{|a_\text{max}|}{\hat\alpha/\hat\beta}
\;=\; \frac{1.99}{g}
\;\approx\; 0.20 \;\text{rad}$$

or about 11.6 degrees. This is a best case static estimate: transient
recovery from a pushed state takes more acceleration to decelerate
$\dot e$ than this static bound suggests, so the realistic region of
attraction is meaningfully smaller. The safety monitor trips at
`control.maxTilt = 2.0 rad`, which is far beyond any recoverable
region, so the safety limit is a catchall for "robot has already
fallen," not a control boundary.

## 12. Implementation Review

### 12.1 Preserved Architectural Boundaries

The rewrite kept every external contract stable:

- class name remains `NonLinearController`,
- the factory (`BalanceControllerFactory.BuildBalanceController`) still
  instantiates it for both the `nonlinear` and the legacy `tanh`
  algorithm aliases,
- `LyapunovController` still exists as a backward compatibility subclass
  that subclasses `NonLinearController` so older imports keep working,
- the `Compute(state) -> ControlOutput` and `Reset()` contracts are
  unchanged,
- left and right commands remain equal (pure sagittal balance),
- the safety monitor remains the final authoritative actuation gate.

Legacy tanh composite variable fields (`kTheta`, `kThetaDot`, `kPhi`,
`kPhiDot`, `sScale`, `thetaDeadband`, `thetaDotDeadband`) remain in the
config schema and in the generated hub runtime mirror so existing YAML
files and previously flashed hub runtime files continue to load, but
the current controller does not read any of them. Removing those
fields would break config parsing on older hub runtimes, so they stay.

### 12.2 MicroPython Safety

The controller is still scalar only. Key details:

- no `numpy`, no arrays, no allocations inside `Compute`;
- `math.tanh` / `math.sin` are resolved once at import time, with
  polynomial fallbacks if the Pybricks build omits them;
- the `_Tanh` fallback uses a Pade [3,3] approximant
  $x(27 + x^2)/(27 + 9x^2)$ with a hard clamp at $|x| \ge 3$ (bounded
  max error approximately 1.2% at $x = 2.5$, dropping rapidly inside
  that range);
- the `_Sin` fallback uses a fifth order Taylor series, which is well
  within tolerance for the tilt range the balance controller ever
  visits before the safety monitor trips.

All invalid state and NaN checks still return a stop command
immediately so the safety monitor never sees garbage.

### 12.3 Construction Time Validation

The `__init__` method validates:

- `wheelRadius`, `bodyMass`, `bodyHeightCoM` must be strictly positive,
- either `bodyInertia > 0` or both `bodyLength > 0` and
  `bodyHeight > 0` must be provided,
- the reduced inertia $J$ and coupling $\hat\beta$ must be strictly
  positive after derivation,
- `innerNaturalFrequency > 0`, `innerDampingRatio >= 0`,
- `surfaceGain >= 0`, `robustGain >= 0`, `boundaryLayerWidth > 0`,
- `outerPositionGain >= 0`, `outerVelocityGain >= 0`,
  `maxReferenceTiltOffset >= 0`,
- `0 <= thetaDotFilterAlpha < 1`.

These validations fail loudly at construction time so that no silent
bad configuration ever reaches the control loop. This is the right
tradeoff for a safety sensitive real time path.

### 12.4 Test Coverage

The updated `tests/test_NonLinearController.py` covers the new logic
specifically:

- invalid state and NaN return stop,
- geometry is read from config and $\hat\alpha$, $\hat\beta$ are
  computed correctly,
- the box inertia fallback triggers when `bodyInertia = 0` and both
  box dimensions are positive,
- construction fails loudly when no inertia source is available,
- the outer loop offset is saturated to `maxReferenceTiltOffset`,
- the inner loop produces a restoring command sign for forward and
  backward lean,
- the actuator inversion correctly maps a desired acceleration through
  `tau / r` and adds it to the current wheel rate,
- saturation clamps at `maxWheelRate`,
- `Reset()` clears both internal states,
- `LyapunovController` still resolves to the same law through the
  compatibility alias,
- the controller still emits symmetric left/right commands.

Legacy tanh composite variable tests that referenced the old state
surface shape have been removed; they were testing an implementation
that no longer exists.

## 13. Tuning Workflow

A practical tuning sequence:

1. **Verify the chassis geometry.** Mass, CoM height, and
   `bodyInertia` drive $\hat\alpha$ and $\hat\beta$. Measure or
   estimate them before touching any gain. This is the single most
   important step; a wrong geometry shifts everything.
2. **Set `targetTilt`** so the robot does not steadily walk in one
   direction with `outerPositionGain = outerVelocityGain = 0`. If
   the IMU zero offset is correct, leaving `targetTilt = 0` is fine.
3. **Start the inner loop** with conservative values such as
   `innerNaturalFrequency = 6.0`, `innerDampingRatio = 1.0`. Increase
   the natural frequency until the robot catches lean reliably.
4. **Adjust the damping ratio** to remove oscillation. Stay near one.
   Heavier damping is safer but slower.
5. **Add a modest `robustGain`** (for example 2.0) and a reasonable
   `boundaryLayerWidth` (for example 0.5 to 0.8). If the robot
   chatters near upright, widen the boundary layer. If the robot
   tolerates pushes poorly, raise the robust gain.
6. **Only then enable the outer loop.** Start with small
   `outerPositionGain` (for example 0.5 rad/m) and a matching
   `outerVelocityGain`. Keep the recentering critically damped in
   terms of the linear position loop.
7. **Leave `maxReferenceTiltOffset`** at a small value (for example
   0.1 rad, about six degrees) so the outer loop can never ask for an
   unreasonable lean.
8. **Use only light `thetaDotFilterAlpha`** if the raw tilt rate is
   noisy. Heavy filtering adds phase lag and erodes stability margin.

### 13.1 Symptom Guide

- **Falls before reacting.** Increase `innerNaturalFrequency`, or if
  that makes the inner loop oscillate, increase `robustGain` instead.
- **Oscillates around upright.** Increase `innerDampingRatio` or widen
  `boundaryLayerWidth`.
- **Too gentle against a push.** Increase `robustGain`.
- **Chatters or buzzes near upright.** Widen `boundaryLayerWidth`, or
  reduce `surfaceGain` so the sliding variable reacts less sharply to
  the tilt error.
- **Balances but slowly walks.** Enable or raise `outerPositionGain`
  and `outerVelocityGain` gradually.
- **Outer loop demands unreasonable lean.** Lower
  `maxReferenceTiltOffset`, or lower `outerPositionGain`.
- **Commands saturate constantly.** Reduce inner loop aggressiveness
  or reconsider the actuator lag and geometry estimates. Hitting
  `maxWheelRate` routinely is a sign that the physics the controller
  is asking for cannot be delivered by the real motor.

## 14. Comparison With The PID Baseline

The repository still ships the discrete PID baseline in
[src/LegoBalance/PidController.py](../src/LegoBalance/PidController.py),
selectable via `controller.algorithm = "pid"`.

| Feature | NonLinearController (geometry aware) | PidController |
|---|---|---|
| Control law | $u = \dot\phi + (\tau/r)(a_\text{nom} + a_\text{rob})$ | $u = K_p e + K_i \Sigma e + K_d \Delta e + K_s \phi$ |
| Physics content | Reduced wheeled pendulum + actuator model | None beyond linear PID |
| State feedback | Full 4 state $(\theta, \dot\theta, \phi, \dot\phi)$, outer loop adds $p$ recentering | 2 state $(\theta, \phi)$, derivative from finite differencing |
| Internal state | IIR filter + previous bounded command | Integral + previous error |
| Saturation | Smooth robust term in boundary layer, hard `maxWheelRate` clamp | External clamp only |
| Actuator lag handling | Explicit first order inversion | None |
| Geometry awareness | $\hat\alpha$, $\hat\beta$ from config | None |
| Wind up behavior | Impossible (no integrator) | Anti wind up clamp required |
| Drift suppression | Separate slow outer loop | Single $K_s \phi$ term |
| Tilt rate source | Gyro (directly from estimator) | Finite difference (noisy) |

The geometry aware law is strictly more physical and strictly more
structured, but the PID baseline is simpler, has no parameter
dependence on mass/inertia/radius, and is useful as a reference
implementation for comparison runs. The factory keeps both available
without any downstream code change.

## 15. Summary Assessment

| Aspect | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Design philosophy | Sound. Geometry aware where the parameters are known, robust correction where they are not. Acceleration based rather than velocity based, which matches the physics of the unstable body. |
| Mathematical structure | Principled. A Lyapunov style nominal law plus smooth sliding mode correction plus an identified actuator inversion. Every term has a physical meaning. |
| Linearized stability | Guaranteed near upright by construction under the current gains. |
| Implementation quality | High. MicroPython safe, scalar only, extensive validation at construction, explicit internal state, clean interfaces, full test coverage on the new logic. |
| Theoretical rigor | Moderate. Nominal Lyapunov descent is real; global proof with the full nonlinear plant and actuator dynamics is not attempted. Appropriate for an educational LEGO platform. |
| Practical readiness | The inner loop is ready; the outer loop is enabled at conservative gains and still being validated on hardware. The robust term is what keeps the closed loop honest against the known parameter uncertainty. |
| Compared to alternatives | Strictly better than the old composite variable tanh law on this hardware (respects the actuator timescale, separates loops cleanly). Strictly better than plain PID for handling the unstable geometry. Deliberately less ambitious than full feedback linearization because the LEGO parameters do not justify exact cancellation. |

The controller is the right shape for this robot: it uses the geometry
it actually knows, refuses to trust the geometry it does not know, and
respects the actuator it has rather than the actuator it wishes it had.

## 16. Open Questions And Honest Limitations

These are the known weaknesses in the current implementation that a
future reviewer should not be surprised by:

1. **Actuator time constant is a rough estimate.** `tau = 0.25 s` was
   inferred from a single motor step response plot, not a least squares
   fit to raw telemetry, and very likely depends on operating point,
   battery state, and loading. The robust correction should be
   interpreted partly as covering this uncertainty, not only covering
   inertia mismatch.
2. **Single motor identification.** The step response used one motor
   with the wheels lifted. The loaded two motor case with a real chassis
   on a real floor has not been directly identified.
3. **Pitch inertia is nominal.** The configured `bodyInertia` is a
   reasonable guess rather than a measured value, and the box fallback
   is an explicit approximation. The robust term must therefore absorb
   inertia uncertainty of order 30% or more.
4. **The outer loop feed forward drops when saturated.** The
   reference derivative is of the unclamped offset. When the saturation
   is active, the inner loop briefly has a small feed forward mismatch.
   This is acceptable given the timescale separation but is not
   mathematically ideal.
5. **The `tau = 0` fallback exists.** If someone forgets to set
   `actuatorTau`, the controller silently degrades to commanding the
   current wheel speed rather than refusing to run. That is a tradeoff
   in favor of "never accidentally brick a hub run" and against
   "fail loud on misconfiguration." It should at least log a warning.
6. **No global Lyapunov proof.** The nominal descent argument is only
   valid near upright and assumes perfect actuation. The controller is
   not proved to be globally stabilizing, and no claim to that effect
   should appear in a formal report without additional work.
7. **No fall recovery.** There is no swing up mode. If the robot
   falls outside the region of attraction, the safety monitor trips and
   the robot stops. Getting it back upright is a human task.
8. **Outer loop gains are still conservative.** `outerPositionGain =
   0.5` rad/m has not been pushed on hardware to find the point where
   the recentering loop starts to interfere with the inner loop. A
   systematic sweep would be useful.

None of these are reasons to avoid the controller. They are just the
honest list of things to mention when someone asks "is this provably
correct?" or "what happens if you push the robot really hard?"

## 17. What To Cite In A Report

If you are writing a report based on this controller, the clean
narrative is:

1. The plant is a sagittal wheeled inverted pendulum with an unstable
   gravitational instability timescale of roughly $1/\sqrt{\hat\alpha}
   \approx 0.1$ s.
2. The actuator is not torque like. It is a first order lag in wheel
   angular velocity with a time constant of roughly 0.2 to 0.25 s,
   identified from a single motor step response.
3. A geometry aware robust nonlinear balance controller stabilizes the
   plant. A slow outer loop generates a bounded tilt reference from
   wheel position and velocity to suppress drift. A fast inner loop
   computes a nominal chassis acceleration from a Lyapunov style law
   derived from the reduced tilt model, plus a smooth sliding mode
   robust correction that absorbs inertia and lag uncertainty. An
   actuator lag inversion maps the desired chassis acceleration into
   the wheel velocity command the motor API actually accepts.
4. The controller preserves every existing repository contract so it
   drops into the existing runtime, estimator, and safety monitor
   pipeline without changing any downstream code.
5. The controller is MicroPython safe and runs unchanged on the SPIKE
   hub through `src/HubPackageBalance.py` and its buffered counterpart
   `src/HubPackageBalanceBuffered.py`.
